**DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS — Working Lands & Industry**

**RILEY:** The AI mineral exploration story is worth spending real time on, because it represents a genuine inflection point in how prospecting works — and BC is squarely in the path of this change.

The traditional model is expensive, slow, and dependent on individual geologists with decades of regional experience. You fly surveys, you core-drill, you miss a lot, you spend years between "we think there might be something here" and "here's a confirmed resource." The new AI-driven approach trains machine learning models on everything that's ever been recorded about a geological region — historical survey data, satellite hyperspectral imagery, geochemical assay results, even archival drill logs that were never digitized — and uses that to generate probability maps of where deposits are most likely to be.

The value proposition is that the model can identify targets that human analysts passed over because the existing data wasn't assembled in a way that made the pattern visible. That's not incremental improvement. That's a different kind of search.

**CASEY:** The technical achievement is real. The question I keep landing on is: what happens to the pace of the rest of the system when exploration accelerates?

BC's environmental assessment process was not designed for a world where a junior company can generate twenty high-probability exploration targets in the time it used to take to evaluate two. UNDRIP consultation requirements, Nation-to-Nation engagement on territory — those processes have minimum timescales that aren't going to compress because a geology model says go fast. You end up with a pressure asymmetry: discovery accelerates, everything downstream doesn't.

**RILEY:** The current pace of exploration is so slow that deposits in BC that could supply critical minerals for the clean energy transition are sitting unmapped while we're importing cobalt from the DRC under far worse governance conditions.
