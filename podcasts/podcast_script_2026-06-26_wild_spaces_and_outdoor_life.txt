But the population going in has changed faster than the judgment baseline has. Someone buys a satellite communicator, downloads a mapping app, and heads into terrain they'd never have attempted fifteen years ago. The communicator gets them rescued. The data doesn't make them competent.

**RILEY:** So the argument is that safety technology creates a moral hazard. More capability, less caution.

**CASEY:** Capability and judgment are separate things, and we've invested almost entirely in capability. There's no equivalent investment in judgment — no widely-used backcountry certification culture in BC the way there is for avalanche terrain, no mandatory route-registration system, no structured trip-planning requirement for remote access. You can just go.

**RILEY:** Avalanche Canada is a counter-example worth taking seriously. The public danger ratings, the education programs, the decision-making frameworks taught in avalanche safety courses — that's a case where the community built something that actually changed how people assess risk, not just how they get rescued after the fact.
