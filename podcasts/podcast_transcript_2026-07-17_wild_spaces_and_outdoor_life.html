Riley: Outside Online ran a piece this week from a travel columnist who says he gets hate mail every time he writes about affordable, uncrowded mountain towns. Readers accuse him of ruining their secret spots. His argument is that trying to keep beautiful places secret doesn't actually protect them, and it's not really possible anyway in an age of geotagged photos and satellite connectivity reaching almost anywhere.

Casey: Easy position to hold when you're not the one living in the town that gets discovered.

Riley: The question that matters for the Cariboo specifically: we're not Bozeman or Canmore — nobody's writing viral listicles about the Chilcotin plateau yet. But satellite internet means someone can post a photo from deep backcountry in real time now. Does that change how these places get used, and how fast?

Casey: It already has. Quesnel Search and Rescue just took in new first aid equipment donations — that tracks with what volunteer rescue organizations across BC have reported about rising call volumes as more people head into remote terrain. That's not a hypothetical future scenario.

Riley: And that's exactly the case for visibility, not against it. More people finding these places through legitimate channels — trail apps, tourism boards, satellite coverage that lets them actually call for help when something goes wrong — means fewer people stumbling in through word of mouth with zero safety net underneath them.

Casey: Or it just means more people arriving, full stop, and the existing safety net stretching thinner. QSAR getting donated first aid kits is not the same as QSAR getting more volunteers or more callout capacity. Equipment isn't throughput.

Riley: Better field equipment does change outcomes in that critical window before a helicopter or ground team arrives, though — that's well-established in wilderness medicine, not a marginal effect.

Casey: Sure, if someone's already been found. The harder problem is volume of incidents, not quality of care once a team is on scene. Volume tracks with visibility. Moab and Squamish both saw rescue call volumes outpace the capacity of their teams within a couple of seasons of getting "discovered" by the outdoor media — and neither had the funding model to catch up.

Riley: Which argues for building capacity ahead of the wave, not for staying quiet and hoping it never arrives. Silence has never actually worked as a management strategy. There are parks in the Rockies that tried downplaying trail conditions and access information for years to manage crowding, and visitation grew anyway — just without the safety messaging that could have gone with it.

Casey: That example proves my point more than yours. Visitation surged without proportional search and rescue investment to match. The lesson isn't "publicity is fine" — it's "publicity without matching infrastructure spending is exactly how you overwhelm a team."

Riley: The wrinkle satellite technology adds — and it matters to be specific — is timing. A few years ago, someone lost or injured deep in the Chilcotin had essentially no way to call for help until they hiked out or someone reported them overdue, sometimes a full day later.
