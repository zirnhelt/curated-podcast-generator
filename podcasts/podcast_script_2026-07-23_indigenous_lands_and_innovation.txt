**DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS - Indigenous Lands & Innovation**

**CASEY:** When a hyperscale data center wants to build on or near a Nation's land, who actually has the authority to say no — not to the dataset six months later, but to the bulldozers before they arrive?

**RILEY:** The reporting that frames this well comes from Grist, syndicated through the Indigenous News Alliance. Hyperscale data centers need enormous amounts of land, huge volumes of water for cooling, and steady cheap electricity — and increasingly they're being sited in rural areas precisely because that's where land and power are both cheap. The Cariboo checks both of those boxes without much effort.

**CASEY:** Which should make this region pay close attention, not treat it as a hypothetical future problem.

**RILEY:** The legal landscape actually differs sharply by Nation. The Tŝilhqot'in title decision from 2014 gives that Nation something most Nations in this country still don't have: declared Aboriginal title, meaning actual jurisdiction to refuse a land use outright, not just a right to be consulted about it.

**CASEY:** Declared title on paper and title enforced against an industrial siting application under provincial energy policy are two very different fights. BC's environmental assessment process runs on its own statutory timeline regardless of who holds title, and a Nation without a declared title ruling — which is most Nations in the Cariboo — negotiates from a materially weaker position than Tŝilhqot'in does.

**RILEY:** [pause:400] Right, and that's the actual stakes here. Most Cariboo Nations are working through impact-benefit agreements and consultation frameworks, not a Supreme Court title declaration. So the real question is what tools exist short of another decade-long court case.

**CASEY:** Structurally, not many. An impact-benefit agreement is a negotiated contract. It can include revenue-sharing, local employment targets, even something that functions like a veto clause on paper. But it only holds as long as both parties keep showing up to the table in good faith. There's no independent body checking five years later whether a data center operator is actually honoring its water-use limits.

**RILEY:** True of most commercial contracts, though — the alternative to imperfect leverage isn't better leverage, it's none at all. A Nation that secures a formal seat in the permitting process before an application is even filed has real influence over siting, cooling method, and water draw. Some Nations elsewhere have reportedly used exactly that sequencing on resource project approvals — engagement locked in before the decision is made, not a comment period bolted on after.

**CASEY:** (wry) "Reportedly" is doing a lot of the reassurance there. Communities like Aamjiwnaang First Nation have had to publicly push for exactly that kind of front-loaded sequencing on nearby industrial projects — which tells you it's still the exception, not the rule.

**RILEY:** [pause:500] It's clearly not universal yet, and that's worth sitting with. But it's a genuinely different kind of lever than the data-ownership fights we usually cover. This is land-use jurisdiction — physical infrastructure, water tables, grid draw — governed by provincial statute and title case law, not by a research ethics framework like OCAP.

**CASEY:** Which means the real test isn't "was the Nation consulted." It's whether the Nation controls the terms of reference for the environmental assessment, the water license conditions, and the decommissioning bond. Those mechanisms are built to survive a change in company ownership or a change in provincial government. A consultation relationship survives neither.

**RILEY:** [overlap:-100] Right.

**RILEY:** It's the same governance question in a completely different domain — not who owns the server, but who gets asked before the story goes up in the first place. **CASEY:** And Wikipedia's whole editorial model is structurally anti-authority. Anyone can edit, consensus among anonymous editors decides what stays — not a designated cultural authority the platform defers to. A guide is a strong recommendation. It doesn't change the platform's actual editing rules. **RILEY:** That's a real gap — it's a policy document, not a technical lock on content.
