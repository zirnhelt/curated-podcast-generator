DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS - Indigenous Lands & Innovation

Casey: The question at the centre of today's theme: being left out of a dataset causes real, measurable harm — that McMaster analysis makes the case plainly, and it's hard to argue with. But being included doesn't automatically fix anything if someone else still owns the questions being asked, the servers where the answers live, and the interpretation of what it all means. So which is actually the bigger problem for a Nation right now — exclusion, or inclusion on somebody else's terms?

Riley: Exclusion is the more urgent one, and it's not close. A drug trial that excludes a population produces dosing guidelines that don't work for that population's bodies. A land-use survey that skips a territory produces environmental baselines that don't account for what's actually growing, spawning, or migrating there. The absence isn't neutral — it actively produces bad outcomes downstream, for decades, because the missing data becomes the assumed default.

Casey: Except getting counted doesn't mean getting served. Indigenous health and land data has been collected for decades by outside institutions and used to justify policy communities never asked for — residential-school-era health surveys, child-welfare risk-scoring models, environmental baselines used to greenlight projects the Nation itself opposed. Inclusion was never the missing ingredient. Control over interpretation was.

Riley: Then the fix isn't refusing participation — it's changing who holds the pen on the research question itself. OCAP exists precisely for this reason. Ownership, control, access, possession — a framework built by Indigenous researchers specifically because the standard clinical-trial model of "we'll include you, thanks for the data" wasn't good enough. It makes inclusion conditional on control, rather than treating the two as opposites.

Casey: OCAP is a strong standard on paper. The gap is enforcement. A hospital in Kamloops or a university lab in Vancouver can cite OCAP in a grant application and still end up owning the servers where the data physically lives, deciding the publication schedule, and controlling which version gets cited when a regulator asks for evidence. Nobody's auditing whether the framework actually gets followed once the funding clears and the press release goes out.

Riley: A real gap — and an argument for building audit capacity, not for treating every dataset as inherently suspect. FirstVoices is the counterexample that matters here. Governance was built into that platform's architecture from day one, not bolted on afterward, and communities still control access to their own language archives years into the project. The model can hold when it's designed right from the start.

Casey: FirstVoices works partly because language archiving doesn't carry the same commercial pull that health data or environmental data does. Nobody's trying to monetize a fluent-speaker recording for an insurance actuary or a mining permit application. Health records and land-use data get pulled into underwriting, resource permitting, policy modelling — there's real money sitting on the other side of that door, and control tends to slip the moment stakes rise.

Riley: A real distinction between the two cases — but it argues for Nations building the analytical capacity in-house, not for staying out of the data collection entirely. CCATEC's training pipelines toward health, resource management, and land administration roles are the concrete mechanism. A Nation that trains its own epidemiologists and GIS technicians doesn't have to hand interpretation over to an outside university lab. The skill floor is the actual lever.

Casey: Training people is necessary. It's not sufficient, and that gap keeps getting skipped over. Secwépemc communities in this region have invested in internal capacity, and they still run into federal and provincial systems that only recognize data formatted and submitted through channels those governments already control. You can train the analyst. You still need the intake system on the other end to actually accept the analyst's version of the answer.

Riley: That's a harder problem than the training gap — it's a recognition problem, not a capacity problem. A Nation can produce its own environmental baseline data for Tŝilhqot'in territory west of the Fraser using its own methodology and its own trained staff, and a provincial permitting process can still default to the baseline collected by a consultant hired by the proponent applying for the permit.

Casey: Extractive inclusion in its purest form. You're not excluded from the process on paper. You're included as one input among several, and the tie always seems to go to whoever the government already has a standing procurement relationship with — regardless of whose data is more current or more locally grounded. It's like being invited to a potluck where someone else already decided the menu.

Riley: Which means the actual lever isn't participation versus non-participation. It's which dataset gets legal standing as the default record when two versions disagree. If a provincial ministry commits, in writing, to treating Nation-collected baseline data as equally admissible to a consultant's report, the entire extraction dynamic shifts without a single new data point being collected. That's a regulatory fix, not a data-collection fix.

Casey: Has that commitment actually been made anywhere in this region, in writing, with enforcement teeth? Because "equally admissible" reads beautifully in a reconciliation press release and quietly disappears the moment a permitting deadline gets tight and the ministry defaults to whichever report landed on the desk first.

Riley: Honest answer — I can't point to a documented case of that commitment surviving under actual deadline pressure in the Cariboo specifically. Which is exactly the test that matters. Not whether the policy exists on a government website somewhere, but whether it holds the first time it becomes genuinely inconvenient for the ministry to honour it.

Casey: That's the research project someone should fund — not another dataset, but an audit of how many times Nation-collected data actually got weighted equally against consultant data when a real permitting decision was on the line, with real money at stake. Nobody particularly wants to run that study, because the answer is uncomfortable for the province no matter which way it lands.

Riley: It would also settle an argument this show keeps approaching from different angles. Right now we're both reasoning from anecdote — the Tŝilhqot'in title case establishing legal decision-making authority back in 2014, FirstVoices holding governance without incident, individual Nations exercising land management authority in specific contexts. A hard number on admissibility outcomes across permitting decisions would tell us whether the exception is becoming the rule, or staying permanently the exception dressed up as progress.

Casey: Until that number exists, the safer working assumption for any Nation is to build data infrastructure a ministry doesn't get to weigh against in the first place — parallel systems, not just parallel inputs feeding into the same external decision-maker who holds the tie-breaking vote.

Riley: Which loops to something concrete a smaller Nation or tribal council in this region could actually try, without waiting on Ottawa or Victoria to move first. A formal data-sharing agreement with a neighbouring health authority or resource ministry that specifies, in advance, exactly which format and methodology gets automatic standing in a decision. Not a general commitment to consultation somewhere down the line — a specific, named technical standard, negotiated before the next permitting cycle opens.

Casey: A workable ask precisely because it's narrow. General reconciliation commitments get quietly reinterpreted every time provincial leadership changes or a new minister takes the file. A signed technical standard is harder to walk back — there's an actual document with an actual definition sitting in a drawer that someone eventually has to point to. Although I'll note that "harder to walk back" and "impossible to walk back" are different animals, and government drawers are surprisingly deep.

Riley: Fair — but the alternative is waiting for a provincial government to spontaneously reform its own intake system, and I wouldn't hold my breath on that timeline. Drafting a technical standard doesn't require new grant funding. It requires legal capacity and negotiating leverage on the Nation's side — which, circling back to CCATEC, is exactly the kind of trained-in-house expertise that lets a Nation write the standard itself rather than accepting whatever template the ministry slides across the table.

Casey: There's a language-and-culture angle that shouldn't get lost under all the permitting talk either, since it's central to today's theme. FirstVoices holds language data because communities insisted on architecture that made outside extraction structurally impossible from day one — not because a funder demanded it. Build the system so the outside institution literally cannot own the record, rather than trusting a policy memo to hold once conditions change. That's the model for land and health data too.

Riley: A genuinely different design philosophy than most rural tech deployments follow. Most broadband or health-tech rollouts start with "deploy the tool, figure out governance later." FirstVoices and OCAP both flip that order — governance decided first, technology built to fit inside it. Slower to launch. Much harder to hollow out afterward.

Casey: Slower to launch is the part that loses funding battles, though. A three-year grant cycle rewards the group that deploys fast and shows a dashboard at the end of year one. It doesn't reward the group that spent year one negotiating a data-sharing agreement and has nothing visual to show a funder yet. "We built a governance framework" doesn't exactly light up a PowerPoint slide.

Riley: Which argues for standing institutional capacity — not project money that resets every cycle, but ongoing operational funding for the negotiating and legal work itself, so a Nation isn't racing a grant clock while trying to get a technical standard drafted properly. That's the forward-leaning version of this: treat governance capacity the same way we treat water treatment or road maintenance — as permanent infrastructure, not a project with a sunset date.
