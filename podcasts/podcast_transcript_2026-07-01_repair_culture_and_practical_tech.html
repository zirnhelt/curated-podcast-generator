DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS - Repair Culture & Practical Tech

Riley: The question worth anchoring on today is a design question, not just a policy question. The regulatory side of right-to-repair gets plenty of attention — legislation, manufacturer pushback, all of it. What gets less attention is whether technology itself can be designed so that repair knowledge is inherently portable and structurally persistent. Not dependent on finding the right person, not dependent on a grant cycle, not dependent on a program that sunsets in three years.

Casey: The Meshtastic story from the roundup is a solid entry point. What makes Meshtastic interesting isn't just the off-grid communication angle. The entire project is open-source, the hardware schematics are public, and version 2.8's GPX export means your data isn't locked inside a proprietary app. That's a design philosophy, not just a feature set.

Riley: And it has a specific Cariboo application. Backcountry users — ranchers checking fencelines, hunters, search-and-rescue volunteers — operate in areas with no cell coverage. A Meshtastic node runs on a TTGO or similar ESP32 board. You can buy the components for under fifty dollars. You can flash the firmware yourself. If a node fails, you replace the board, not a subscription.

Casey: The skill floor doesn't disappear just because the hardware is cheap. Flashing firmware requires knowing what firmware is. Sourcing an ESP32 in Horsefly is not the same as sourcing one in Vancouver.

Riley: Shipping exists.

Casey: Shipping takes a week, and you might need the node tomorrow. The Cariboo isn't just geographically remote — it's logistically remote in a way that compounds every repair timeline. When the nearest electronics supplier is three hours away, the buffer between 'it works' and 'we're waiting on parts' is razor thin.

Riley: Which is exactly why the design philosophy matters more here than anywhere else. Communities that can't run to a store need gear that's designed to be understood, maintained, and improvised around. The Meshtastic codebase has an active community — people who've documented repairs, workarounds, edge cases. That documentation is a form of infrastructure.

Casey: Documentation that assumes internet access to retrieve it. Real circularity problem for off-grid applications.

Riley: Though Meshtastic 2.8's offline mapping improvements are addressing that. The GPX export isn't just about sharing routes — it's about keeping data usable without a cloud service. Portable formats, open standards, local-first operation. That's the pattern.

Casey: The retrocomputing story from Hackaday lands here too. Riley: Different capability level, but it maps to something real in rural communities. Rural mechanics have been doing functional equivalents of this for decades. That's not makeshift — that's applied engineering. The retrocomputing community is rediscovering something rural workshops never stopped doing.
