**DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS - Working Lands & Industry**

**RILEY:** [pause:500] The Bunker Hill mine in Idaho's Silver Valley just shipped its first ore after six years of reconstruction. That's a historic operation — the original mine ran for over a century before it was shut down, and the cleanup from its contamination became one of the largest Superfund sites in the United States. Now a Canadian company, Bunker Hill Mining, has rebuilt the mill and is ramping up silver production.

**CASEY:** The Superfund cleanup cost was borne by taxpayers, not the original operators. That's the part that gets skipped when people tell the resurrection narrative.

**RILEY:** The legacy contamination is real and it matters. What interests me here is the ownership and value-capture structure on the rebuild. Bunker Hill Mining is TSX-listed — so it's a Canadian public company extracting silver in Idaho, presumably with American processing contracts and American labour. The value flows in multiple directions and the community connection depends entirely on how the royalty and employment structures are set up.

**CASEY:** Which is the problem. A TSX listing doesn't tell you whether the town of Kellogg, Idaho sees meaningful long-term economic benefit or whether it sees a temporary construction boom and then a skeleton-crew operation that exports concentrate to a smelter in another jurisdiction.

**RILEY:** The mine-to-mill-to-smelter chain has always been where value bleeds out of resource communities. The question is whether ownership models are emerging that actually change that. And some are worth examining.

**CASEY:** Name one that's worked at scale in a community comparable to anything in the Cariboo.

**RILEY:** The Osisko Mining development in Malartic, Quebec is the closest analog — a mine developed with explicit community benefit agreements, a reclamation fund built into the operating structure from day one, and a portion of the royalty stream directed to a local foundation. Not perfect, not radical redistribution, but structurally different from a pure extraction lease.

**CASEY:** Malartic also displaced a residential neighbourhood to expand the open pit. The community benefit agreement came after significant local conflict, not before. So the model exists, but the leverage that produced it was community resistance — not a voluntary design choice by the developer.

**RILEY:** Which means the lesson isn't "trust developers to build good structures" — it's that the structures emerge when communities have sufficient political leverage to demand them before the permits are signed.

**CASEY:** And in the Cariboo, who has that leverage right now?

**RILEY:** The Nations do. The Tŝilhqot'in Supreme Court decision fundamentally changed the consultation and consent requirements for resource development on Aboriginal title land. Any new mining or forestry project in that territory has to work through a governance process that can actually condition the terms of development. The Tŝilhqot'in National Government has been exercising that authority on multiple fronts.

**CASEY:** On title lands, yes. A significant portion of the Cariboo's working landscape sits on Crown land under provincial jurisdiction, where the duty to consult exists but the power to set conditions is weaker. The Xatśūll First Nation and the Williams Lake First Nation are both navigating resource development questions in areas where their title claims are asserted but not yet determined. That's a different negotiating position than the Tŝilhqot'in's post-2014 reality.

**RILEY:** So the leverage varies by Nation and by the specific legal status of the land. The value-capture question doesn't have one answer for the Cariboo — it has several, depending on where you're standing.

**CASEY:** That's a more useful frame than "does the Cariboo benefit from mining," because the real question is which communities, under what legal frameworks, with what negotiating capacity.

**RILEY:** The hatchery story from the 100 Mile Free Press is a useful contrast. The Clearwater trout hatchery stocked more than 2.5 million fish into BC lakes last year. That's a public infrastructure function — provincially funded, ecologically significant, and it supports a recreational fishing economy that generates real revenue for outfitters, guides, and tourism operators across the South Cariboo.

**CASEY:** Almost none of that value is captured by the hatchery itself or by the communities adjacent to the stocked lakes. The guide outfitters who benefit most are often not locally owned. The province funds the hatchery as a public good and the downstream economic benefit disperses without a clear mechanism to direct it back into the communities doing the work of maintaining that ecological productivity.

**RILEY:** The hatchery model is striking because it's a public asset generating private value downstream without a royalty or licensing structure that recaptures any of it. A fishing lodge on a lake stocked by public hatchery funds is essentially receiving a subsidy without a fee attached.

**CASEY:** Nobody's going to say that out loud at a fishing lodge. But the parallel to forestry is exact — Crown timber harvested on public land, milled by private operators, value captured at the mill and above, with stumpage fees that have historically been set below market rates for the standing timber.

**RILEY:** Stumpage reform has been politically impossible for decades, but the mechanism that changes it might not be political pressure — it might be the carbon market. Once you can price the standing tree as a carbon asset, the stumpage calculation has a competitor. A First Nation that can choose between harvesting and a carbon credit has real optionality that a non-title holder doesn't.

**CASEY:** The carbon market optionality is real, but generating meaningful credit volumes requires a certain scale of forested land, plus navigating a verification and registry system that is genuinely complex. The Tŝilhqot'in have done it. Whether smaller Nations with smaller land bases can replicate that without significant technical capacity investment is a separate question.

**RILEY:** That's the capacity gap that keeps surfacing — not whether the tools or mechanisms exist, but whether the people who should be using them have the time and institutional bandwidth to navigate them.

**CASEY:** And whether the solutions being offered are designed for their scale, or whether they're provincial and federal programs built around large operators and awkwardly scaled down.

**RILEY:** [pause:500] So what's actually different now compared to, say, 2015? The structural critiques you're raising are not new. The value-bleed problem in resource communities has been documented for decades.

**CASEY:** Two things are genuinely different. First, the legal landscape post-Tŝilhqot'in is materially changed — consent requirements have teeth they didn't before. A ranch in the Chilcotin is now potentially a carbon asset, a biodiversity offset candidate, and a site for range-scale renewable development on top of its beef production. Multiple income streams from the same land base changes the negotiating leverage for operators who understand how to stack them.

**RILEY:** The income-stacking model is where the most interesting working-lands conversations are happening right now. And the verification cost for range-scale carbon credits isn't trivial — you need soil sampling, satellite monitoring, and third-party audit, all of which eat into the credit value before the producer sees a dollar.

**RILEY:** Which is where a producer cooperative structure matters — aggregate the verification cost across fifty ranches and the per-unit cost drops to something manageable. **RILEY:** [pause:400] That's the funding treadmill problem. And the question worth pushing is whether there's a structural way off it, rather than just securing better grants.

**CASEY:** Because "we need more stable funding" sounds like action without changing anything.

**RILEY:** One model that gets off the treadmill is direct community ownership of the verification infrastructure itself. If a First Nation or a producer cooperative owns the soil sampling equipment, trains its own staff to run the protocol, and holds the registry account rather than contracting it out, the dependency on external service providers disappears over time. Slower to build — but not hostage to the next government's grant priorities.

**CASEY:** That's a real path. It requires capital investment upfront and a multi-year commitment to skills retention, which is hard in communities with high turnover. **RILEY:** The Williams Lake First Nation has been building exactly that kind of internal capacity in forestry management — not just participating in forestry agreements, but developing the planning and monitoring functions in-house. **CASEY:** [pause:400] The distinction between holding a contract and holding the capacity to execute the contract is the actual difference between temporary benefit and permanent change. A company can lose a contract. Capacity doesn't disappear the same way. That part I think is right.

**RILEY:** And that's the frame worth applying to every working-lands investment question in the Cariboo right now — not "what project is being funded" but "what capacity is being built, by whom, and does it stay in the community when the project is done."
