**DEEP DIVE: CARIBOO CONNECTIONS — Community Tech & Governance**

**RILEY:** So today's central question. We've covered a lot of ground on this theme over the past several weeks — governance failures that push communities into litigation, information infrastructure and who controls emergency alerts, monitoring authority and data sovereignty. What we haven't fully wrestled with is this: what does it mean for a community to *govern* technology, as opposed to simply *using* it? And specifically — what does that look like for Indigenous nations in BC who are building their own digital infrastructure?

**CASEY:** The Animikii material from today's roundup gives us a concrete entry point. There's a distinction worth drawing between two kinds of community tech governance. The first is a community using technology to run a better town council — digital permits, online consultations, e-voting for local decisions. The second is a community building and controlling the technological systems that shape their own governance. The Niiwin platform is squarely in that second category.

**RILEY:** And that second category is genuinely harder. Building a data governance platform requires technical capacity, legal frameworks, sustained funding, and community trust — all simultaneously. Animikii's approach of embedding team members inside communities is a meaningful design choice. It acknowledges that technology systems reflect the values and priorities of whoever designs them. If the designers are outsiders, the system embeds outsider assumptions.

**CASEY:** Which sounds obvious when you say it, but it's violated constantly. Most "digital services for Indigenous communities" projects have been built by provincial or federal contractors who'd never set foot in the community, based on requirements gathered in a two-day workshop. The result is systems that communities are expected to maintain and trust, built on someone else's model of what they need.

**RILEY:** So Animikii addresses the design problem. But even a perfectly community-centred platform still needs people inside the community who can administer it, update it, troubleshoot it, and evolve it as needs change. What does the capacity-building piece actually look like?

**CASEY:** This is where I get genuinely concerned about sustainability. We've raised this in other contexts — digital governance platforms have a familiar failure mode. A nation might have a well-designed, community-controlled Niiwin deployment today. Three years from now, the grant that funded the engagement has ended, the one person who understood the system has moved to Kamloops, and nobody can update the permissions structure when membership rolls change. That's not a hypothetical — it's a pattern that repeats across Indigenous digital infrastructure projects across the country.

**RILEY:** That failure mode is real. But I'd push back on framing it as unique to Indigenous-led infrastructure. The sovereignty design and the maintenance dependency can directly work against each other.
