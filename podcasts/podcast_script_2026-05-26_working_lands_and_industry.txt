**CASEY:** The Narwhal ran the other half of that story this week. Canadian farmland prices have risen dramatically over the past decade — partly climate, partly tariff pressure, partly the simple fact that cultivable land is finite — and increasingly, the most financially rational decision a farmer can make is to sell. Not because they want to leave. Because the asset value of the land has decoupled from the productive value of farming it.

**RILEY:** That decoupling is the core problem. When land as a financial instrument outperforms land as a working operation, the market pressure runs in one direction.

**CASEY:** And it's not hypothetical. The Narwhal piece documents institutional investors — pension funds, asset managers — buying agricultural land as a store of value. They're not farming it, or they're leasing it back to the previous owner under terms that make the former owner a tenant. The family farm doesn't disappear visually. It just changes its legal and economic structure underneath.

**RILEY:** Some of those sale-leaseback arrangements actually give producers operating capital they couldn't access through conventional ag lending. If your land is worth several million dollars but the bank will only loan against projected crop yields, selling to an investor and leasing back can unlock capital for equipment, infrastructure, transition investments. It's not uniformly predatory.

**CASEY:** The power asymmetry compounds over time, though. The investor can exit. The farmer can't — not without losing the operation entirely. When the lease comes up for renewal, who has leverage? We've seen this pattern in other sectors where asset ownership and operational expertise get separated — the operator becomes expendable once the knowledge is transferred.

**RILEY:** That's the structural trap. And it connects directly to the ranch story. What the NYT piece is describing — whether the editors framed it this way or not — is a model where the land stays in mission-aligned hands because the ownership structure was built to prevent that financial decoupling. The ranch isn't a school that happens to have land. It's a land operation that uses mentorship as a mechanism for succession and continuity.

**CASEY:** So the innovation isn't the training program. It's the governance structure that keeps the training program possible.
